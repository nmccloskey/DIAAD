# Transcript Text Normalization and Speaker Exclusion Research Context

Text normalization and speaker exclusion are small settings with large interpretive consequences. They define what counts as comparable speech and whose language enters an analysis.

## Normalization As A Reliability Choice

Reliability comparisons can be distorted by trivial notation differences. Lowercasing, removing CLAN markup, and choosing how to handle correction notation can help focus the comparison on substantive transcript differences.

Those choices should match the goal of the reliability check. If the goal is to compare the recoverable lexical content of two transcripts, normalization is usually appropriate. If the goal is to evaluate adherence to a detailed transcription convention, stripping markup may hide differences that matter.

## Speaker Exclusion As An Analytic Boundary

Speaker exclusion defines the language sample. In monologic narrative tasks, this may be simple: the participant's speech is the target. In clinician-client or conversational tasks, the decision is more interpretive. Excluding prompts may be necessary for participant-level lexical measures, while including partner turns may be necessary for interactional measures.

The same transcript can legitimately support different analyses with different speaker-inclusion rules. What matters is that the rule is explicit and consistent within a reported analysis.

## Reporting Implications

When results depend on these settings, report enough detail that another analyst can reproduce the analytic text:

- whether CLAN markup was stripped;
- whether correction notation preferred corrected forms;
- whether text was lowercased;
- which speaker labels were excluded;
- whether exclusion happened before coding-file generation, before analysis, or both.
