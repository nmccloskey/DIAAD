# Monologic Narrative Word Counting Research Context

Word counts are simple to name but not always simple to define. In discourse-analysis workflows, a word count usually reflects a protocol for deciding what counts and what should be omitted.

## Human Review Of Automated Counts

DIAAD's first-pass counts can reduce workload, but they should be treated as starting values for human review. This is especially important in samples with aphasia-related disfluencies, repetitions, fillers, nonwords, neologisms, and task commentary.

Protocols influenced by Nicholas and Brookshire-style rules may selectively omit material that a generic text counter would include. Those decisions should be documented in the project protocol.

## Difference From Target Vocabulary Coverage

Word Counting measures total countable language. Target Vocabulary Coverage measures production of words from a predefined lexicon. A sample can be long while covering little of a target lexicon, or short while covering many required target items.

This difference matters for reporting. Words per minute and target-vocabulary tokens per minute are rate-normalized counts, but they describe different constructs.

## Reliability

Word-count reliability is useful because many disagreements are small but systematic. Differences may reflect protocol ambiguity, missed omissions, transcript errors, or coder fatigue. The detailed reliability workbook should be inspected before treating a summary metric as a final decision.

## Rates

Words per minute depends on the quality of the speaking-time value. Report how speaking time was measured and whether non-target speakers or non-countable utterances were excluded.
