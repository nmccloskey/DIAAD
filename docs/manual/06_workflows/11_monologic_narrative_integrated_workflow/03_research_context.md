# Monologic Narrative Integrated Workflow Research Context

An integrated monologic narrative workflow treats a discourse sample as the basis for several complementary observations. Complete Utterances, word counts, and Target Vocabulary Coverage are related because they all begin from transcript-derived utterances, but they do not measure the same construct.

## Complementary Measures

Complete Utterance coding organizes manual judgments about utterance completeness under a project protocol. Word Counting estimates how much countable language a sample contains after human review. Target Vocabulary Coverage scores a sample against an active resource of expected or task-relevant vocabulary.

Using these measures together can be useful because a sample may be fluent but not cover target vocabulary, lexically rich but not organized into complete utterances, or brief but efficient relative to speaking time. The interpretation comes from the pattern, not from treating one output as a replacement for another.

## Reliability Across Manual Steps

Integrated workflows multiply the need for clear reliability documentation. Transcription, CU coding, and word counting can each introduce different kinds of error. DIAAD can select reliability samples, organize reliability workbooks, and summarize agreement, but the project must decide which reliability standards fit its protocol and research aims.

Reselection should be treated as a documented additional reliability round, not as a hidden cleanup step.

## Blinding And Practical Identifiability

Identifier blinding can reduce bias during manual coding and later analysis. It cannot remove identifying content from transcript text, and it cannot prevent recognition when coders are familiar with the sample context. For many projects, the most useful pattern is to encode before manual coding, decode before DIAAD analysis, and re-encode selected exports only when needed.

## Speaking-Time Interpretation

Per-minute rates help compare samples with different lengths, but the numerator still matters. A word-per-minute rate, a complete-utterance-per-minute rate, and a target-token-per-minute rate should be interpreted as module-specific summaries rather than interchangeable fluency measures.
